# Build Guide B: Risk and Opportunity Management

## 1. What this process is for

This process keeps uncertainty attached to objectives. It identifies what could hinder or help each objective, assesses those items on calibrated scales, decides treatment against a stated appetite and verifies that treatments exist and work. It manages opportunity with the same discipline as risk, because a register that only records downside describes half the organization's uncertainty.

## 2. Elements invoked

Core: **A2 Objectives → A3 Identification → A4 Assessment → A5 Design**, the ALIGN pipeline. Upstream: **L1/L2** (context feeds identification), **A1** (appetite is the yardstick). Downstream: **P1 Controls** (treatments become controls), **R1 Monitoring** (ratings set watch thresholds), **R3 Improvement**.

## 3. Process design

| Step | Owner | Input | Output | Cadence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Confirm objectives | Executive owner | Objectives register | Current register with owners, measures, tolerances | Annual + on change |
| 2. Identify per objective | GRC function facilitates; unit management contributes | Objectives, context briefs, event history | Opportunities, obstacles and obligations recorded per objective with source | Semiannual full pass + rolling |
| 3. Route obligations | GRC function | Identified obligations | Entries decomposed into the obligations register (guide C) | Rolling |
| 4. Assess | Item owners with GRC calibration | Calibrated scales, control matrix | Inherent and residual ratings, velocity, control effectiveness | Semiannual + on change |
| 5. Evaluate vs appetite | Executive owner | Assessed register, appetite framework | Prioritized queue; items above appetite flagged for decision | Per assessment pass |
| 6. Decide treatment | Accountable executive | Prioritized queue | Documented decision per item: reduce, avoid, transfer, accept (risks); pursue, enhance, share, decline (opportunities) | Per queue item |
| 7. Design treatment | A5 owner | Treatment decisions | Treatment plans with owners, resources, dates; controls into the control matrix | Per decision |
| 8. Implement and evidence | Unit management | Treatment plans | Operating treatments with evidence (P1) | Per plan |
| 9. Monitor | R1 owner | Ratings, tolerances | Indicators and thresholds per priority item; breaches escalated | Continuous |
| 10. Review cadence by rating | Item owners | Register | High rated items reviewed quarterly, medium semiannually, low annually | Tiered |

## 4. System requirements (tool-agnostic)

1. **One register** holding risks and opportunities with objective linkage mandatory on every row (see `../04-templates/risk-register.md`).
2. **Calibrated scales stored as configuration**, not tribal knowledge: the impact, likelihood and velocity definitions live in the system and print on every report. Example anchoring for a five-point impact scale: define each level in money, time, people and reputation terms specific to your organization; do the same for likelihood in frequency or probability bands. Calibration means two trained assessors give the same item the same score.
3. **Inherent and residual views** with the control identifiers that explain the difference.
4. **Appetite comparison** rendered on every item: above, at or within appetite, with above-appetite-no-decision as a standing exception report.
5. **Decision log**: who decided what treatment, when, on what rationale. Acceptance without a name is not acceptance.
6. **Treatment tracking** to closure with evidence links.
7. **History**: rating changes over time per item, because trajectory informs more than position.

## 6. Measures and the REVIEW loop

R1 indicators: items above appetite without a decision (target zero), assessment currency, treatment plan on-time rate, opportunity share of the register, calibration drift between assessor groups. R2 samples: ratings against evidence, decision log completeness, whether high-rated items received their tiered reviews. R3 receives systemic findings: chronic assessment inflation or deflation is a calibration or incentive problem, not an arithmetic one.

## 7. Failure modes

1. **Risk lists divorced from objectives.** A register organized by category instead of by objective drifts into generic content. Objective linkage is mandatory at entry, not retrofitted.
2. **Heat map theater.** Color as the product. A heat map is a sorting aid; the products are decisions and treatments. If the map updates and the decision log does not, the process is decorative.
3. **The eternal amber.** Items above appetite reviewed forever, decided never. The standing exception report in section 4 exists to make this visible and uncomfortable.
4. **Assessment inflation and deflation.** Scores drift toward whatever the incentive rewards: inflation to win resources, deflation to avoid attention. Calibration sessions and cross-unit comparison are the countermeasure.
5. **Opportunity blindness.** Only downside is logged, so the capability reads as the department of no. Identification workshops ask both questions of every objective: what could hurt this and what could accelerate it.

## 8. Reading the equation against this build

This is Ω's ALIGN pipeline at full depth, conditioned typically by SP2 (a risk discipline integrating) or SP0. The Universal Outcomes most exercised: U2 (balance risk and reward, the opportunity half included) and U5 (integrated decision-making, the decision log as evidence). The equation's test: if assessments are current but decisions are absent, C is operating while UO shows no motion, and the honest reading is a sorting machine, not a management process.
