# Principled Performance

Principled Performance® is the goal of the entire method: the reliable achievement of objectives while addressing uncertainty and acting with integrity. The definition is OCEG's; this document unpacks it into operating terms.

The definition carries three commitments, and the method fails if any one is dropped:

1. **Achieve objectives.** GRC is not a brake bolted onto the business. It exists to make the organization's objectives more likely to be achieved, and it must be able to show that linkage. Any GRC activity that cannot name the objective it serves is a candidate for removal.
2. **Address uncertainty.** Objectives live in an uncertain world. Uncertainty produces both obstacles (which threaten objectives) and opportunities (which advance them). Addressing uncertainty means both: managing downside and enabling deliberate pursuit of upside, against a stated appetite.
3. **Act with integrity.** The organization meets its mandatory obligations and honors its voluntary commitments and values, even when no one is watching and even when it costs something. Integrity is what keeps achievement from being purchased with conduct that destroys value later.

Treating these as one integrated discipline rather than three silos is the point of the model. A risk function that never sees objectives manages the wrong risks. A compliance function that never sees strategy becomes a paper factory. A performance function that never sees obligations wins quarters and loses licenses.

## The ten Universal Outcomes

The Universal Outcomes are the direction of aspiration in the equation (**↑ UO**). They are also the honest test of whether the capability works. For each outcome: what it demands, what evidence looks like and the common failure mode.

**U1. Achieve objectives that produce and preserve value.**
Demands: strategy and execution prioritize objectives that create value and protect it at the same time.
Evidence: the objectives register shows both growth and preservation objectives, each with an owner and measures.
Failure mode: value production crowds out preservation until an avoidable loss erases years of gains.

**U2. Balance risk and reward.**
Demands: decisions weigh opportunity and obstacle on comparable terms against a stated appetite.
Evidence: treatment decisions reference appetite; an opportunity view exists alongside the risk view.
Failure mode: pure downside management. Growth decisions bypass the capability entirely, so the register describes a company that does not exist.

**U3. Improve culture.**
Demands: a climate of performance, accountability, integrity, trust and open communication.
Evidence: speak-up volume with low retaliation signals, survey trend lines, leadership behavior consistent with stated values.
Failure mode: values on the wall, incentives on the floor. People read the incentives.

**U4. Enhance stakeholder confidence.**
Demands: stakeholders can rely on what the organization says about its performance, risk and conduct.
Evidence: assurance results support external statements; commitments made are tracked to closure.
Failure mode: confidence built on assertion rather than evidence, which holds until the first test.

**U5. Integrate and improve decision-making.**
Demands: governance, management and assurance of performance, risk and compliance inform decisions as one picture.
Evidence: decision papers show objective, risk and obligation context together; decision criteria trace to direction (A1).
Failure mode: three reports on the same subject that disagree, so leadership picks the convenient one.

**U6. Prevent, detect and correct undesired conduct and weaknesses.**
Demands: layered actions and controls that stop what can be stopped, find what gets through quickly and fix what is found.
Evidence: a control matrix with all three types operating; shrinking detection-to-correction time.
Failure mode: prevention theater with no detection, so the organization believes it has no problems because it cannot see them.

**U7. Promote, detect and reward desired conduct and strengths.**
Demands: the capability works the positive side: it notices, amplifies and compounds what goes right.
Evidence: recognition tied to conduct and not only to results; strengths identified in reviews feed design.
Failure mode: a capability that only punishes teaches people to hide everything, good and bad alike.

**U8. Sense and respond to context.**
Demands: the organization detects internal and external change early enough to adjust strategy and tactics deliberately.
Evidence: a live watchlist with triggers; documented adjustments that cite a sensed change.
Failure mode: annual scanning in a monthly world. Change arrives as surprise and is handled as crisis.

**U9. Improve total performance.**
Demands: effectiveness, efficiency, agility and resilience improve together through proactive, detective and responsive action.
Evidence: improvement backlog throughput; the same finding does not recur year over year.
Failure mode: audit findings as an annual ritual, closed on paper and reopened in practice.

**U10. Honor and express values.**
Demands: the pursuit of performance never requires the organization to become something it claims not to be.
Evidence: documented decisions where a profitable option was declined on values grounds; values referenced in design choices.
Failure mode: values invoked only in marketing, priced at zero in every real trade-off.

## Controlled vocabulary

Working definitions for this repository. Where your organization already maintains a controlled vocabulary, the organization's definitions govern; record the mapping.

1. **Objective:** a measurable outcome the organization commits to achieve, with an owner, measures, targets and tolerances.
2. **Opportunity:** an uncertain event or condition that would help achieve an objective if pursued or captured.
3. **Obstacle (risk):** an uncertain event or condition that would hinder an objective if it occurred. Risk is the effect of uncertainty on objectives.
4. **Obligation:** a requirement the organization must meet, whether mandatory (statute, regulation, contract, order) or voluntary (standard adopted, public commitment, internal code).
5. **Requirement:** a specific, decomposed statement of what an obligation demands in context, assignable to an owner.
6. **Appetite / tolerance / capacity:** appetite is the amount and type of uncertainty the organization is willing to take in pursuit of objectives; tolerance is the acceptable variation around a specific objective or measure; capacity is the maximum it could absorb and survive.
7. **Control:** a management action, device or process designed to address an opportunity, obstacle or obligation. Controls are proactive (prevent or promote), detective (find promptly) or responsive (correct and recover).
8. **Policy / standard / procedure:** a policy states what and why at the level of principle; a standard states the required specifics; a procedure states how, step by step.
9. **Event / issue / incident:** an event is anything that happens and is noticed; an issue is an event assessed as requiring action; an incident is an issue involving actual or probable harm, loss or violation.
10. **Assurance:** an objective examination of evidence to provide a reliable conclusion about design and operating effectiveness.
11. **Evidence:** records sufficient for a competent third party to reach the same conclusion.
12. **Finding:** a documented gap between what should be true and what the evidence shows, with significance rated.
